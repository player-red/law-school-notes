Introduction
- Shift from foraging to farming -> First Neolithic Revolution (VG Childe)
- Humans being able to control the reproduction of plant and animal species, select desirable characteristics -> Domestication
	- First occurred in the Levant (10,000 BC): beginning of Neolithic period.
- Changes that occurred in the Mesolithic period came together to form the new society of the Neolithic period.

Childe's Climate Change Theory and Similar Theories
- External shocks such as animal extinction, climate change and induced transformations of ecosystems.
- 'Oasis theory': originally lived in an environment capable of supporting their basic needs, then a major climate change occurred (Pleistocene -> Holocene) and this 'oasis' turned into an arid desert unsuitable for life. 
- Forced to migrate to areas where life was still possible, like banks of large rivers. 
- Plants and animals were also forced to do the same, and the familiarity with plants and animals allowed humans to understand their growth cycles and begin agriculture (food production).
Criticism:
- Does not explain why agriculture wasn't invented before this time. 
- Assumes that humans did not have knowledge of plants and animals prior to the Holocene period.
	- Evidence proves otherwise. 
Similar theory:
- Regions with high/low climatic volatility evolve slower than regions with moderate climatic volatility. 
- Development of garden agriculture in New Guinea disproves this (low volatility).

Adaptation and Resilience of Hunter-Gatherer Societies
- Traditional theories attributed the origin of agriculture to the failure of foraging systems due to climatic deterioration. 
- Neolithic revolution was the first example of social collapse.
- Challenged by HG societies living in the Levant, contending that it was a simple shift in the resource focus, and not a collapse of forager (resilient and adaptable). 
- Predomestication cultivation would have been an option for foragers, and accumulated experiences were needed for responding to economic challenges. 
- Nine similar events occurred, but only the last one led to the development of agriculture. 

Childe's Proto-Agriculture Theory and Braidwood's Nuclear Zones About How Agriculture Developed
- Childe
	- May have developed from proto-agriculture in Eurasian and North African places. 
	- Alluvial soil: most suitable for cultivation. 
	- Horticulture: preparing the soil for planting.
	- Controls competition from intrusive plants and provides protection from predatory animals. 
	- Crop often stored for future consumption after harvesting and processing. 
	- Garden: permanent location -> forced humans to settle down. 
	- Adoption accelerated by invasions and climate change.
	- Explains how humans transitioned to agriculture, but not why. Does not explain how proto-agriculture developed.
- Braidwood (Nuclear Zones Theory)
	- Domestication would have arisen in nuclear zones/hilly flanks (natural habitats of ancestors of domesticated plants and animals).
	- Believed that the climatic criss did not occur. 
	- Explains how and where agriculture took place, but not why it developed. 

Population Pressure Theories
- Growing population provided impetus for the development of agriculture. 
- Need for food supplies led to the adoption of agriculture.

Marginal Zone Theory
- Binford: cyclical population pressure on the edges of the Nuclear Zones.
- Assumes that humans exist in equilibrium with the environment, and do not normally intensify their food supplies. 
- Population growth due to local environmental disruption meant people moved to the Marginal Zones (hilly flanks) which were suboptimal for food resources.
- Created a systemic imbalance since there were inadequate wild food resources for all, and hence agriculture began to improve this balance. 

An Infinite and Irreversible Evolution
- Two stages of economic development: subsistence (survival) then more productive methods such as agriculture (in order to feed the increasing population) that led to maximizing resources.
- Malthusian theory of population.

Impact of Hunter-Gatherers' Migration
- Tribes living in areas where proto-agriculture began had their hunting-gathering territory taken away by invading tribes.
- Hence developed garden agriculture and migrated and likely displaced locals.

Is Food Deprivation a Necessary Condition for Agriculture's Commencement?
- All previous theories based on a 'push' factor that essentially forces these societies to develop agriculture. 
- Population pressure is generally the reason behind the transition to agriculture. 
- Scholars argued that many groups had various methods of population control and never reached Malthusian limits. 
- No archeological evidence of food crises prior to the development of agriculture. 
- Criticism of Childe's theory: agriculture could have been a response to better climatic conditions, since ecosystems could support more diverse species.
	- This might have led early humans to settle and have more children, somewhat like a reverse of the Malthusian theory. 

Abundance and Social Rivalry Stimulate Agriculture Development
- Excess demand for food can exist even if there is no population pressure. 
- Engel's laws of consumption: when income increases, consumption shifts from primary to luxury goods
- Social competition for prestige were endogenous and this led to conscious adaptation of agriculture.
Criticism:
- Does not explain the underlying causes of competitive feasting.
- Does not explain the development of agriculture.
- Surplus needed for feasting was an outcome of agriculture, not the cause. 
- Assumes that farming was a desirable job even in the early stages of agricultural development.
	- Used to be a low-return, high-risk activity.

Low Attractiveness of the Farming Way of Life
- HG were not mobile and did not have simple societies.
- LBK culture was a mixed economy of both hunting-gathering and agriculture.
- Deliberate choice to switch to agriculture due to cultural diffusion does not have much support.
- HG society: original affluent society since they did not have to work much to satisfy their needs.
- Agriculture was more time-consuming, labourious, more likely to cause sickness and malnutrition, more capricious, etc.
- Reversion to hunting and gathering also occurred (native American tribes).

Livestock and Agricultural Development
- Food production does not immediately supersede food-gathering, since food production requires gathering at first by default.
- Domestication of animals was also slow.

From Taming to Domestication: A Long-Term Process
- Long process that involves ecological, biological and cultural factors.
- Two polar cases:
	- Humans acting as prey against nature
	- Husbanding by domestication of plants and animals
- Taming is not morphological/biological modification of species, domestication is. 
- Taming activities are forms of proto-agriculture, and led to domestication in Nuclear Zones.
- Non-shattering seed is thought to have been an trait evolved as a byproduct of growth and natural selection, and it took about 2000-4000 years to develop. 

A Mixed Economy Based on Foraging and Farming
- Domestication by itself cannot support a transition from HG to agriculture.
- Almost no economic surplus was yielded by agriculture, it was mostly a support to the main income. 
- The development of irrigation and the plough (5900 and 4500 BCE in Mesopotamia), invention of the wheel, heat-tolerant strains of crops allowed the people to cultivate surplus food.

Diverse Reasons for Agriculture's Commencement in Different Centres
- Neolithic revolution occurred independently in seven different locations.
- Requirements: climate conditions supportive of cultivation, appearance of wild plants or animals suited to domestication. 
	- Australia had neither, so neither agriculture nor animal husbandry developed.

Natural resources, Institutions and the Beginnings of Agriculture
- Does economic development depend more on nature or on culture?
- Two schools of thought:
	- Natural resource endowments
	- Institutions
Natural Resource Endowments View
- Economic development varied based on differences in geographic and biogeographic conditions.
- Certain plants and animals can only be be cultivated and bred within tolerance limits.
- Diffusion of agriculture hindered by difficult terrain, disease, etc.
Institution View
- Inclusive institutions facilitated growth, whereas extractive ones led to crisis, economic and social collapses.
Mutual Causation
- Critical resources other than food also existed (for example, stone for making stone tools).
- Human capital accumulation and transmission of knowledge through generations were also necessary (making systems necessary).
- Core of a center: economic development first, slowed down later due to extractive systems.
- Periphery of a center: economic development later, because they were inclusive from the beginning.
