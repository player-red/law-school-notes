- Classical Greek social theorists were interested in the causes and consequences of political and material inequality, and their effects on economic growth. 
- This paper is concerned with research on the actual material conditions during which these theorists wrote.

2.1 Economic Growth and Comparative Performance
legitimately the most useless reading in the course: argues that greece was one of the most "equal" democracies and its logic was that only among the rich, landed citizens (MEN) had a gini coefficient of 0.38 or smth so the 'democracy' was an equal one

- 1000 - 300 BCE: population of Greece grew from 330k to 8250k.
- Compared to most premodern economies, it did very well. 
- Life expectancy increased, due to good nutrition. 
- No of people living in urban areas was high for the premodern period.

2.2 Inequality
- Boix's theory: economic development drives political development. Calls the Greek case an 'alternative path to development'.
- Athens an outlier among premodern societies' Inequality Extraction Ratio: due to low income inequality and high consumption rates.
- Level of nutrition did not vary much among the different economic classes and slaves.
- House sizes: indicators of wealth inequality and consumption?
	- Bell curve distribution of house sizes.

2.3 A Theory of High Growth with Low Inequality
- Rule egalitarianism -> economic growth. 
- "Fair rules and fierce competition" hypothesis
- Economic development was a result of many micro-level choices that each individual took + collective decisions that added up. 

2.4 History
- Incentive stronger when it is intrinsic (when you work for yourself and your own ambition).
- Public insurance and redistributive taxes.
- Standardized weights and measures, open access to information and institutions.
- Institutional innovations tested within city-states and ideas shared among city-states.
