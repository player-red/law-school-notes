I
- The invention of the clock and its spread throughout the common people led to a new notion of time, one of immediacy and omnipresence.
- It became part of people's daily lives to be acutely aware of time (wind-up clocks were common and winding them up became part of people's daily routines).
- Examining how this shift in the idea of time affected workers and labourers — their ideas of work, discipline and nature.

II
- Among primitive people, time was measured by their cycles of work and domestic chores.
- Some professions were required to follow their notions of time: seafarers had to measure by the tides, hunters by certain hours to set traps.
- Called task-oriented time.
	- More easy to understand (human) than timed labour.
	- Shows least separation between work and life.
	- For people who are used to working by the clock, it appears slow and inefficient.
- When wage-labourers are employed, a shift from task-oriented time to timed labour occurs.
	- Creates a distinction between the employer's time and the time that the labourers have to themselves.
	- Time becomes currency, and is spent rather than passed.
	- Monotony of tasks and alienation from the pleasure in working. 

III
- Clock towers were often used to notify people of the time, and sound functioned much better than sight.
- Attempt to levy a tax on owning clocks and watches (justification that it was an article owned by the well-off).
	- Impossible to collect.
	- Large-scale buyers' strike, reducing economic growth. 
- Led to general diffusion of cheaper clocks (made out of wood as well) just when the Industrial Revolution demanded synchronization of labour. 
- It was seen as an investment, and something to buy once a worker had some amount of surplus income.

IV
- Task orientation was still prevalent in manufacturing when conducted on a small scale.
- Irregularity of labour patterns: many labourers worked at multiple different tasks, ones not related to their main profession. 
	- Bouts of intense labour then idleness, especially when people were in control of their schedules.
	- Still present in self-employed people today: is it the natural rhythm of working?
- Monday was often a day of idleness (called Saint Monday) because of the holiday feeling that carried over from the weekend (people drank heavily on the weekends). People used it to take in and give out work, or for their personal work. 
- The advent of machinery meant the beginning of discipline. 
- Wives of labourers and farmers had it the hardest: they not only took care of infants and children, but also returned to field work once they were able. 
	- The irregular, task-oriented schedule of a mother remains to this day.

V
- Transition from this form of work to industrial capitalism demanded synchronization of labour and exact time management.
- Mercantile classes used wage labour to prevent "idleness" as part of the irregular work cycles.
- Time sheets documenting the amount of time workers took to do their tasks began appearing.
- Systems of clocking-in were created, and time-keepers were hired.
- Masters wished for their workers to rise early, go to bed on time (reducing "midnight revelry"), to save their money and not "waste" time by doing anything like loitering in the markets or celebrating festivals.
	- Made use of pamphlets, but more importantly charity schools that taught the children the very qualities these masters were hoping for (punctuality, frugality, discipline).
	- They thought of schooling as training in the habit of industry, to acclimate children to the rough and arduous nature of labour. 
- Some employers would cheat their workers and squeeze a few more minutes out of their breaks, or even not allow workers to bring watches and make them work as many hours as possible for less pay.

VI
- Puritan ethic: more insistence on hard work and punctuality.
- Someone who was rich had "made good use of his time". Time began to become a currency.

VII
- Division and supervision of labour, incentives, time-keeping, schooling, etc. led to the formation of new labour habits and time-discipline. 
- Under capitalism, for the labour force to simply pass the time by doing nothing was not allowed, they would have to constantly consume and be marketed to.
- People from different cultures were discriminated against (as labourers) since their work-culture was not a capitalist industrial one, and they had more ties to the old task-oriented system.

VIII
- Leisure has now become a problem to solve.
- If using time is less compulsive, we have to relearn how to use our time as we used to in the days of task-oriented work.
- Economic growth = change of culture.
