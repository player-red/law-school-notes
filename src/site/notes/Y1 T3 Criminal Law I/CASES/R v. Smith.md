Facts:
- Respondent a soldier charged with the murder of another regiment's soldier during a fight. 
- Sergeant-major kept all the men there on parade to learn who had been involved in the fight. 
- Respondent confessed to the stabbing at the parade.
- One more confession made to the sergeant of the SIB. 
- Deceased dropped twice on the way to the reception station.
- Deceased given incorrect treatment at reception station. 
- Deceased died shortly after. 

Arguments of respondent
- Appellant claiming that the confessions were made under threat, and were hence wrongly admitted in evidence. 
- Also claimed [[Y1 T3 Criminal Law I/CONCEPTS/Novus actus interveniens\|Novus actus interveniens]], because the deceased was dropped twice and incorrectly treated before his death.

Arguments of R
- Even if wrongful act of third party occurred, it could reasonably be anticipated that a man would die due to a stab injury. 
- Death was the sole consequence of the wound. It is unreasonable to expect immediate proper medical care. 
- Argued that the confession (1st) was admissible because the sergeant's words did not amount to a threat. 

Issues:
1. Whether the negligence on the part of the doctors was enough to break the chain of causation and clear the accused of his liability towards the victim?

Decision:
- The stab wound was an operating cause of the victim's death, it does not matter if it was not the sole cause. 
- The chain of causation was not broken: in order to break the chain of causation, the act must be one that disturbs the sequence of events and is unreasonable and extrinsic. 
	- If the new act is so overwhelming that it makes the original act a simple part of history, then we can claim novus actus interveniens.
- The 2nd confession was deemed admissible since the threat had expired by the time the 2nd confession had been made.
