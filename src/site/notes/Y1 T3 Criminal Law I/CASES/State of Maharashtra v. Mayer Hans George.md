Statute(s) used for this case: Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1947 and Sea Customs Act, 1878.

Facts:
- George was a German national who was engaged in the trade and transportation of gold from one country to another.
- Had a special jacket with 28 pockets that could carry large amounts of gold. 
- He was on an incoming flight from Zurich, and stopped at Santacruz Airport in Mumbai to take a flight to Manila.
- Airport authorities stopped him and asked about the gold, to which he replied "What gold?"
- They found 34 kilograms of gold in his jacket, which had 28 pockets, and the respondent denied having any gold. 
- Two important RBI notifications involved in this case:
	- 25th August: no one would be allowed to bring gold into India without general or special permission. However, general permission was granted to carry gold through transit to any place outside Indian territory. 
	- 24th November: it was made mandatory to declare gold carried in "cargo".
Presidency Magistrate convicted -> Bombay HC acquitted -> case is now in the SC.

Issues:
1. Whether [[Y1 T3 Criminal Law I/CONCEPTS/Mens rea\|Mens rea]] is necessary for the offence under Section 23(1-A) of the FERA, 1947?
2. Whether the respondent would be held guilty of the offence of bringing gold to India under Sections 8(1) and 23(1-A) of the FERA?

Arguments of the parties
Appellant (State of Maharashtra):
- Mens rea is not necessary under the FERA since it was enacted to prevent smuggling, which means that the act is enough to convict.
- Declaring the gold was made compulsory by the 2nd notification (8th Nov) — not declaring the gold was a contravention of the provisions and a violation of Sections 8 and 23(1-A) of the FERA.
- Had only the first notification applied, the respondent would not be guilty. However, the 2nd notification made what the respondent did a prohibited act (carrying gold without declaring it).

Respondent (Mayer Hans George)
- Mens rea is an essential element of a criminal offence, and the respondent requested that it be considered for the interpretation of this statutory offence as well. 
	- This is because it is a common presumption that mens rea is necessary for a statutory offence.
- Said he did not have to declare the gold since he was carrying it on his person, and not in cargo. 
- Notification released by delegated legislation, and no provision for the publication of the notification was mentioned, so he had no knowledge of the offence: "ignorantia juris non excusat" does not apply, according to him. 

Court's judgement:
- Convicted (majority)
- Held that mens rea is not essential. Voluntary bringing of the gold was enough to constitute an offence. 
- Burden of proof on the accused to prove that he had the permit under FERA.

Dissenting judgement:
- if the burden of proof was on the accused, the knowledge of the crime was essential. 
- Mens rea is essential for conviction under this statute.
	- Just because the FERA promotes social welfare/public policy, does not mean mens rea becomes non-essential.
